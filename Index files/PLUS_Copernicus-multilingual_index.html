Copernicus

Owing to the legacy of Nikolaus Kopernikus, Copernicus is the name of the European Earth observation programme. Next to Galileo, Copernicus is the flagship of the European Space Policy. It comprises Space infrastructure, an integrated ground segment and a dedicated portfolio for so-called information services. Copernicus is a key strategic element in the emerging Earth observation / Geospatial (EO*GI) sector.

Nikolaus Kopernikus (1473 – 1543) was a Renaissance mathematician and astronomer, who formulated a model of the universe that placed the Sun rather than Earth at its center [Wikipedia] (Photo: S Lang)

Space Assets

The peaceful use of Outer Space is a one of the great achievements of humankind. Today, there is an increasing number of pivate commercial activites in Space. There are three main space assets:

  • Satellite Navigation (SatNav)

  • Satellite Communication (SatCom)

  • Satellite (Earth) Observation (SatObs)

[1] Satellite Navigation (SatNav)

The European SatNav programme is called Galileo. SatNav technology comprises positioning, navigation and timing (PNT). More then twenty operating satellites guarantee location-based services for various applications. Together with EGNOS, the European differential global positioning system, Galileo belongs to the world spanning global navigation satellite systems (GNSS).


Galileo-enabled smartphones can be used in many different applications.

[2] Satellite Communication (SatCom)

SatCom allows telecommunication all around the globe. Communication satellites receive signals from Earth and retransmit them by a transponder.


The satellite as a self-contained communications system, the example of IntelSat.

[3] Earth Observation (EO)

Earth observation is the systematic usage of satellite remote sensing technology for continuous or on-demand imaging and measuring of the Earth surface. EO satellites operate since more than 40 years in sun-synchronous or geo-stationary mode (e.g. weather satellites).


A view from the Cupola, the observation deck of the ISS. Photograph by astronaut A. Geerst.

The Copernicus Idea

Copernicus comprises three major technical components. What is specific to this space programme is a comprehensive space data exploitation strategy, including the establishment of main (previously: core) information services and downstream services.

  • Space component

  • Ground segment

  • Information services


Space Component

The Space component refers to the infrastructure of satellite missions in orbit. The so-called Sentinel satellites are the backbone of the Copernicus EO space component.

Integrated Ground Segment

The Copernicus ground segment includes the network of ground receiving stations and the satellite data storage systems. In addition, it comprises the in-situ observation network, the contributing missions, as well as access to the Copernicus services. It is therefore called integrated ground segment.


Copernicus integrated ground segment (IGS)

Data and Information Access Service (DIAS)

To facilitate and standardize access to data, cloud-based platforms provide access to Copernicus data and information products, as well as to processing tools. The five so-called DIAS platforms allow users to discover, manipulate, process and download data and products. A competitive funding scheme by the European Commission has stimulated different profiles and prorities among the DIAS.


The (currently) five Copernicus DIAS

Beyond Space Assets and Data

The main driver of Copernicus is to support global policies and conventions and EU regulations and directives with a defined portfolio of geospatial information services serving specific benefit areas


Copernicus Services

There are six Copernicus services addressing major thematic areas. Earlier, these priority areas were called core services highlighting their general significance.

  • Land monitoring

  • Ocean monitoring

  • Atmosphere monitoring

  • Climate change

  • Emergency management

  • Human security

Land Monitoring (CLMS)

The Copernicus Land Monitoring Service aims at monitoring the land surface. There are four service components, the global, the pan-European, and the local component as well as reference data.

The Copernicus land monitoring service portfolio. Source: European Commission

Ocean Monitoring (CMEMS)

The Copernicus Marine Environment Monitoring Service observes the global ocean.

The Copernicus marine environment monitoring service portfolio. Source: European Commission

Atmosphere Monitoring (CAMS)

The Copernicus Marine Environment Monitoring Service focuses on the conditions of our atmosphere.

The Copernicus atmosphere monitoring service portfolio. Source: European Commission

Climate Change Service (C3)

The Copernicus Climate Change Service focuses on climate change and related measures. The focus is on essential climate variables (ECV), mitigation and adaptation strategies, and forecasts

The Copernicus climate change monitoring service portfolio. Source: European Commission

Emergency Management (CEMS)

The Copernicus Emergency Management Service focuses on emergency response. It has two components, rapid mapping and risk & recovery

The Copernicus emergency management service portfolio. Source: European Commission

Human Security

The Copernicus Security Service focuses on human security. It consists of three components: border surveillance, maritime surveillance, support to EU external action.

The Copernicus security service portfolio. Source: European Commission

Validation

The geospatial information products undergo a rigorous validation process. It includes, broadly speaking, technical validation and user validation. Technical validation comprises aspects of quality assurance (QA) of the actual information product, such as as (1) reliability of the information content; (2) usability (incl. readability); (3) consistency in representation; (4) efficiency (incl. delivery time, update). User validation refers to the appraisal of the products by clients and the uptake in established business processes.

Data Providers

Next to the Sentinel satellites there is a series of contributing missions, including commercial providers of very high (spatial) resolution (VHR) data.


Copernicus contributing mission, e.g. commercial satellite data provider; here: Worldview-2 by Maxar (formerly DigitalGlobe)

Service Providers

The standardised information products are usually provided commercial bidding consortia. Regular procurement procedures guarantee a fair competition. In addition, private companies develop and offer tailor-made products in the emerging downstream services sector.

A conceptualisation of a downstream service in the agricultural application domain, using Open Data Cube, Copernicus and EGNOS data. Idea proposed by a Kenyan start-up at the EU Space Week 2020.

Users and Stakeholders

Copernicus User Uptake activities address all potential user groups, from public authorities to NGOs, from business partners to ultimately every citizen. Next to excellence, quality, and technical versatility, trust-building and communication are major cornerstones of the process towards a marketable and competitive product. The matching triangle between the user/client side, the adaptability of the service provider, and the agility of the academic backbone, has to meet the fine line between understanding existing user needs and demand creation.

To overcome barriers between the private vs. the public sector, the industry vs. the academic sector, is a long process but key to the success of Copernicus.

Copernicus Academy

The Copernicus Academy comprises academic institutions, research organisations, public authorities, and industry and is creating a stimulating and open atmosphere to share ideas and solutions.

The foundation of the Copernicus Academy was laid in 2012 by the Salzburg Declaration.

Distributed Capacities

The Copernicus Academy is a network of more than 160 organisations worldwide, based on the principles of active exchange, open innovation, and trust. The distributed capacities can be visualised in a dynamic knowledge landscape

Data & Information Access

The Copernicus programme enables access to standardised data and information products. For this purpose, interoperable (geospatial) web services are key.


Data vs. information

Interoperable Web Services and Specifications

Web services as defined by the Open Geospatial Consortium (OGC) enable geospatial information to be sourced from the web, independent of the software environment or browser. Interoperable web services such as WMS, WCS, etc. are distinguished according to certain usage scenarios and related capabilities (viewing/background, analysis, etc.)
